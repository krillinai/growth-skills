# Portfolio, Sequencing, And Scenarios

## Classify Commitments

| Portfolio class | Purpose | Evidence and governance expectation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core | Protect or deepen validated customer value and the current economic engine | Strongest evidence; monitor degradation, saturation, and concentration |
| Adjacent | Extend a validated capability, customer, use case, product, channel, or market | Explicit transfer assumptions; bounded pilot and graduation gates |
| Exploratory | Resolve a strategic uncertainty or create an option | Small, reversible commitment; learning milestone and expiry |
| System | Build shared data, delivery, infrastructure, governance, or organization capability | Named downstream decisions, adoption owner, reliability and retirement plan |

Do not impose a universal allocation ratio. Record capacity in compatible units such as accountable people, specialist time, cash, management attention, data or engineering capacity, partner bandwidth, and operational risk. Preserve reserved capacity for core obligations, maintenance, trust, compliance, and unexpected change.

## Build The Portfolio Register

For every bet record:

- portfolio class, thesis, customer and business outcome;
- strategic assumption and evidence state;
- owner, decision rights, capacity request, and opportunity cost;
- required capabilities and upstream or downstream dependencies;
- earliest decision-changing evidence and maturity date;
- economics, cash timing, capacity effect, and guardrails;
- commitment level: observe, research, prototype, pilot, scale, hold, graduate, pause, or retire;
- graduation, scale, cap, stop, fallback, rollback, expiry, and next-review rules.

Do not label routine work as exploratory to avoid accountability or call an unvalidated large commitment a pilot. A pilot must bound customer exposure, cost, duration, decision, harm, and reversibility.

## Sequence By Dependency And Learning

Sequence commitments using these rules:

1. Fix definitions, identity, safety, trust, or capability gaps that make downstream evidence unreliable.
2. Acquire discriminating evidence before choices that depend on it.
3. Prove customer value and repeated value before distribution or monetization scale where the model requires them.
4. Test the narrowest coherent market, segment, product, or mechanism before broad transfer.
5. Make reversible commitments before irreversible commitments.
6. Scale only when retained value, marginal economics, capacity, and guardrails remain acceptable.
7. Stop, pause, or reframe bets whose thesis, economics, or prerequisites no longer hold.

Parallelize only when capacity and dependencies allow it and the tests answer distinct decisions. More initiatives do not create diversification when they share the same platform, channel, customer, data, or operating dependency.

## Validate Economics And Capacity

Keep users, customers, accounts, seats, transactions, orders, revenue, GMV, gross profit, contribution, cash, and valuation separate. Keep average and marginal economics separate. Include acquisition, onboarding, delivery, incentives, payment, support, success, sales, partner, compliance, data, infrastructure, model inference, refunds, fraud, and opportunity costs where relevant.

Do not backsolve conversion, retention, margin, payback, LTV, price, or capacity to satisfy a target. Use attributable actuals, explicit assumptions, bounded ranges, sensitivity, downside, maturity, and reconciliation. Route detailed models to `unit-economics-analysis`, `ltv-analysis`, `growth-forecasting`, and `growth-model-design`.

Capacity is a strategic constraint. Connect customer volume and complexity to product, engineering, sales, support, fulfillment, moderation, security, finance, legal or regulatory review, partner, and leadership capacity. State where growth would reduce quality, trust, reliability, or contribution.

## Build Conditional Scenarios

Create scenarios only for uncertainties that change a choice, sequence, or commitment. Examples include retention strength, marginal acquisition cost, category adoption, competitor response, platform access, regulation, pricing power, supply liquidity, capacity, and capital availability.

| Scenario field | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Condition | Named external or internal state, not “good,” “base,” or “bad” alone |
| Assumptions | Internally coherent and compatible definitions |
| Evidence | Supporting, contradictory, unavailable, and freshness limits |
| Strategic effect | Choice, portfolio, sequence, economics, capacity, and guardrail impact |
| Trigger | Observable leading signal and threshold or qualitative condition |
| Response | Retain, accelerate, cap, hedge, preserve, pause, switch, or retire |
| Owner and date | Accountable observer and next review |

Do not invent scenario probabilities, average conditional futures into an expected strategy, or call a scenario a forecast. Use calibrated probabilities only when separately supplied by a valid forecasting process. Identify:

- **robust commitments** that remain sensible across plausible scenarios;
- **contingent commitments** activated by an observable trigger;
- **hedges** that bound material downside;
- **options** that preserve future choice at acceptable cost;
- **irreversible commitments** that require the strongest evidence and approval.

## Test Portfolio Coherence

Before delivery, check that bets do not compete for the same unavailable capability, target mutually inconsistent customers or positioning, create conflicting pricing or channel incentives, overload lifecycle stages, duplicate evidence, hide shared concentration risk, or violate explicit exclusions. Show unresolved conflict instead of forcing a coherent narrative.
