# Growth Organization Methods

## Constraint-To-Structure Test

Before selecting a model, answer:

1. Which customer and business outcome is bounded and accountable?
2. Which supplied evidence identifies the current constraint, and what alternatives remain?
3. Which decisions and levers can materially affect it?
4. Which product, engineering, data, design, distribution, commercial, operational, local, and risk capabilities are required?
5. Which work is contextual, and which is repeated and stable enough to share?
6. Which decisions are reversible, shared, irreversible, regulated, financial, employment-related, or high-risk?
7. Which owner can operate the mandate and maintain what it creates?
8. What duration, handoff, review, adjustment, dissolution, or reorganization trigger applies?

If the constraint is unavailable, create the organization evidence request and route diagnosis. Do not use an org-chart choice to hide uncertainty.

## Model Comparison

| Model | Evidence required | Failure signal | Design response |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Central outcome team | concentrated constraint, product access, cross-functional work, bounded duration | executive ticket queue, detached local optimization, permanent ownership | narrow intake, named outcome, protected capacity, handoff and dissolution |
| Embedded ownership | decisions materially depend on product or customer context | duplicated metrics, experiments, delivery, risk controls, or staffing gaps | shared contracts and services with local decision rights |
| Growth-as-a-Service | bounded temporary specialist need and receiving owner | recurring dependency, weak transfer, unclear accountability | time-box, transfer artifact, recipient capability and completion gate |
| Autonomous mission unit | major outcome, dedicated surfaces, high decision frequency | core-product conflict, duplicated infrastructure, unowned maintenance | interface, shared guardrails, system ownership and review date |
| Center of excellence | repeated method and quality need across capable local teams | manual approval queue, policy volume, low adoption | self-service, risk tiers, coaching, tooling and decision-quality measures |
| Platform-plus-local | stable common service plus meaningful product or market variation | platform dominance, local bypasses, unclear exceptions | service contract, configuration, override, escalation and roadmap feedback |

## Decision-Rights Matrix

For each decision, record:

| Decision | Class and reversibility | Proposer | Decider | Executor | Required consultation | Reviewer | Stop authority | Escalation and time bound | Record |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

One role may hold several rights, but no decision should have multiple final deciders. Define thresholds that move a decision between classes. Broad input should improve judgment without creating implicit vetoes.

## Shared Versus Local Boundary

Share only when repeated use, stable inputs and outputs, common quality or governance, real consumers, reliable ownership, support, and lower total coordination cost are evidenced. Typical shared candidates include identity, semantic contracts, assignment, delivery rails, security, privacy, audit, documentation, and training.

Keep customer problem, product judgment, market message, creative choice, local channel operation, commercial context, and final action local when context changes the decision. A shared model, score, or platform can inform a local decision without owning it. Define configuration, exceptions, overrides, logs, escalation, cost allocation, and feedback.

## Staffing Method

For every role or capability scenario, state:

- work and outcome served;
- required decisions, depth, and expected load;
- existing coverage and actual gap;
- partner and management capacity;
- ongoing operations, maintenance, support, and on-call work;
- employment type or temporary alternative as a scenario, not a directive;
- evidence-based start, expand, redesign, and stop triggers;
- realistic evaluation evidence and work sample.

Do not use generic growth titles, fixed ratios, prestige, or copied large-company teams. A small team may need broad people with one deep specialty; specialization adds handoffs and management cost.

## Organization Change Test

A reorganization requires a named decision-flow mismatch, alternatives, expected improvement, transition cost, affected customers and people, permissions and knowledge transfer, unfinished work, system ownership, communication, validation horizon, harm measures, and adjustment or rollback. A missed target can trigger diagnosis, not automatically a reporting-line change.
