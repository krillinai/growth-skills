# Customer Research Methods And Sampling

## Match Method To Decision

| Method | Best use | Main limitation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Problem or decision interview | Triggers, alternatives, workflows, constraints, consequences, and decision criteria | Recall, selection, and interviewer effects |
| Contextual observation | Actual workflow, environment, handoffs, and workarounds | Access, privacy, observer effects, and limited coverage |
| Win/loss or churn review | Buying, rejection, switching, cancellation, and alternative evidence | Post-rationalization and reachable-customer bias |
| Support or sales coding | Recurring language, friction, objections, and operational patterns | Contact population differs from all customers |
| Public review analysis | Public language, expectations, alternatives, and failure reports | Extreme-experience, platform, identity, and authenticity bias |
| Survey | Bounded prevalence, prioritization, or structured comparison | Wording, sampling, nonresponse, and hypothetical bias |
| Behavioral or economic join | Whether reported patterns appear in actions or outcomes | Measurement does not explain motives by itself |

Use mixed methods when explanation and prevalence or behavior must both inform the decision. Do not use a survey to discover a poorly defined construct and then treat forced-choice answers as customer truth.

## Sampling

Choose a sampling frame that can distinguish the decision's hypotheses. Use purposive variation across relevant job, role, market, value outcome, activation, retention, conversion, churn, alternative, or buying state. Include negative and disconfirming cases.

Convenience samples, internal champions, highly active users, public reviewers, reachable churned customers, and survey respondents each have selection limits. Record who could not be reached, who declined, who was excluded, and which relevant conditions are absent.

Do not use a universal interview count. Set an initial target by decision diversity and operating constraints, review whether new evidence changes the model, and expand where contradictions, missing cells, or high-risk decisions remain. Saturation is bounded to the sampled range and current question.

## Screener And Recruitment

Screen on observable conditions available before the interview. Avoid revealing the preferred answer or recruiting only people who already display the desired outcome. Separate role, account fit, lifecycle state, product behavior, buying authority, and market.

State incentive amount or range, eligibility, payment owner, timing, tax or platform questions, and whether incentive could distort participation. Incentives compensate time; they do not purchase favorable answers.

## Interview Guide

Move from context to concrete past behavior:

1. establish the participant's role, situation, and relevant job;
2. reconstruct the last real occurrence and trigger;
3. identify prior alternatives, workarounds, sequence, effort, and constraints;
4. examine outcome, consequence, confidence, and what happened next;
5. explore buying, adoption, switching, or stopping decisions when relevant;
6. test counterexamples and changed circumstances;
7. ask for clarifications before solutions or hypothetical intent.

Avoid questions such as "Would you use feature X?", "How valuable is our easy workflow?", or "Would you pay $Y?" without behavior and context. Do not debate, sell, teach, or repair the participant's answer during evidence collection.

## Surveys

Define population, sampling frame, invitation method, response window, construct, wording, order, randomization, scale, required and optional questions, quality checks, missingness, weighting, and analysis before launch. Report invitations, starts, completions, exclusions, and item denominators.

Do not infer population prevalence from an open link, customer newsletter, social poll, or convenience panel without a defensible frame. Treat stated intent and willingness to pay as directional unless validated against behavior.
