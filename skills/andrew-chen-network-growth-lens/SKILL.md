# Andrew Chen Network Growth Lens

Apply Andrew Chen's publicly documented methods to network products, marketplaces, and growth systems. Treat this as a method lens, not an impersonation, endorsement, or substitute for first-party evidence.

## Establish Applicability

Determine whether product value depends on interactions among participants, shared content, inventory, collaboration, or local density. Do not force network-effect frameworks onto a product that delivers complete value to a single user.

Use known context. Ask only for consequential gaps: product, participant roles, core interaction, atomic-network boundary, hard side, geography or category, stage, activation event, repeat interval, match or success rate, retention, incentives, acquisition mix, economics, and current constraint.

Keep unknowns explicit. Never invent company metrics, market structure, participant behavior, or Andrew Chen's opinion.

## Select the Lens

Read [frameworks.md](references/frameworks.md). Choose one primary lens and no more than two supporting lenses.

| Situation | Primary lens |
| --- | --- |
| The product has users but weak interaction value | Atomic network and density |
| One participant group limits the other | Hard-side constraint |
| A marketplace cannot complete enough transactions | Liquidity system |
| The first market works but expansion stalls | Network-effects ladder |
| Invites or sharing create traffic but not compounding value | Retention-adjusted growth loop |
| Paid CAC or CTR deteriorates with scale | Channel decay and paid dependence |
| A new feature is proposed to fix weak engagement | Lifecycle reach |
| Blended engagement hides user behavior | Power User Curve |
| Team ownership does not match the bottleneck | Constraint-led growth organization |

## Build the Diagnosis

1. Define the participant value outcome and decision scope.
2. Map the selected lens using user evidence.
3. Label claims `verified`, `inferred`, or `missing`.
4. Separate aggregate scale from local density, interaction success, and retained value.
5. Compare constraints by impact, evidence, reach, control, learning value, economics, and ecosystem risk.
6. Select one primary constraint while preserving alternatives and contradictory evidence.
7. Recommend the smallest 30-day actions that can change a decision.
8. Define an experiment or evidence plan with population, intervention, primary metric, guardrails, maturity, and ship/iterate/stop rule.
9. Name where this lens may underweight non-network product value, brand, regulation, sales motion, or operational realities.

Do not jump from “network effects” to referral tactics. Signups do not establish density; invitations do not establish a loop; incentives do not establish durable supply; total GMV does not establish liquidity; and paid-channel decay does not prove the product should abandon paid acquisition.

## Deliver

Use this output order:

1. **Scope and participant value**
2. **Lens applicability**
3. **Network or lifecycle map**
4. **Primary constraint and alternatives**
5. **Supporting, contradictory, and missing evidence**
6. **Three prioritized 30-day actions**
7. **Decision-oriented experiment or evidence plan**
8. **Metrics and guardrails**
9. **Blind spots and alternative lens**
10. **Sources**

Read [sources.md](references/sources.md) before citing. Distinguish Chen's writing and talks from guest essays, coauthored work, third-party summaries, and company-wide outcomes.

## Required Boundary

Include this concise disclosure in substantial outputs:

> This analysis applies frameworks documented in Andrew Chen's public work. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or a simulation of Andrew Chen.

Never write as Andrew Chen, imitate his voice, imply private knowledge, or claim he would make a decision.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating total users as evidence of local density or interaction quality.
- Assuming supply is always the hard side without testing scarcity and motivation.
- Copying Uber's incentives or city operations into a different market.
- Measuring gross invitations without recipient value, retention, and cycle closure.
- Reading a smile-shaped Power User Curve as a universal product target.
- Treating a historical benchmark, guest article, or third-party summary as Chen's current view.
- Attributing a company's growth to one person or framework.
