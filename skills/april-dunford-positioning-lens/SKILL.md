# April Dunford Positioning Lens

Apply April Dunford's public positioning methods to clarify why a product matters, to whom, and relative to what. This is a method lens, not an impersonation, endorsement, or substitute for customer evidence.

## Establish the Positioning Unit

Define product or portfolio, geography, route to market, buyer, user, use case, stage, current alternatives, claimed capabilities, customer outcomes, segment evidence, sales motion, pricing, existing category, and decision. Ask only for consequential gaps.

Mark unknowns explicitly. Never invent customer language, product capabilities, proof, market categories, company facts, or Dunford's views.

## Select the Lens

Read [frameworks.md](references/frameworks.md). Choose one primary and at most two supporting lenses.

| Symptom | Primary lens |

| Competitor analysis includes only similar vendors | Competitive-alternative map |
| Features are listed but customers do not understand why they matter | Capability-to-value chain |

| Leadership confuses positioning, strategy, and vision | Decision-layer separation |

| Discovery calls do not identify fit | Positioning-led qualification |
| Several products produce an incoherent umbrella message | Portfolio positioning architecture |
| The company wants to create a new category | Category-creation burden test |
| AI claims sound identical to competitors | AI positioning evidence audit |

## Build the Position

1. State the product, best available customer evidence, and decision scope.
2. Map actual competitive alternatives, including inaction and internal workarounds.
3. Identify capabilities alternatives cannot match under relevant conditions.
4. Translate each capability into consequence, customer value, and proof.
5. Identify customers who care urgently and can realize the value.
6. Choose the category that provides the most helpful truthful context.
7. Label each claim `verified`, `inferred`, or `missing`.
8. Preserve contradictory evidence and alternative positions.
9. Translate the position into narrative, qualification, and execution tests.

Do not generate a final tagline, category, or value proposition from unsupported assumptions. Do not hide competitors by redefining the market, use a trend as the position, or claim uniqueness without evidence.

## Required Boundary

Include this disclosure in substantial outputs:

> This analysis applies frameworks documented in April Dunford's public work. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or a simulation of April Dunford.

Never write as Dunford, imitate her voice, imply private knowledge, or claim she would choose a position.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating positioning as copywriting or brand tone.
- Starting with a category, persona, or slogan instead of alternatives.
- Calling a feature valuable without explaining its customer consequence.
- Defining the target as everyone who could use the product.
- Creating a category without estimating education cost and strategic control.
- Testing tagline preference instead of acquisition, sales, adoption, and retention behavior.
