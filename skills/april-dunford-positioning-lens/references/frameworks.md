# Framework Reference

## Five-Component Positioning

Work in dependency order:

```text
competitive alternatives
-> unique capabilities
-> differentiated value
-> best-fit customers
-> market category
-> relevant trends
```

Use trends only after value is clear. The output is a set of evidence-backed decisions, not a slogan.

## Competitive-Alternative Map

Ask what customers would do if the product did not exist. Include direct products, general tools, spreadsheets, internal development, manual processes, agencies, and inaction. Segment alternatives by use case and customer state. Validate through interviews, win/loss, replacement behavior, and sales calls.

## Capability-to-Value Chain

For each claimed difference, record:

| Element | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Capability | What can the product do that the alternative cannot? |
| Condition | When is the difference true and relevant? |
| Consequence | What changes in the customer's work or outcome? |
| Value | Why does the customer care? |
| Proof | What evidence supports the claim? |

Reject generic benefits and roadmap-only differentiation.

## Best-Fit Customer Definition

Describe customers by problem urgency, current alternative, workflow, constraints, company or user state, required capabilities, buying process, willingness to pay, adoption conditions, and retained outcomes. Prefer behavioral and situational evidence over demographic breadth.

## Market-Category Choice

Compare candidate categories by buyer familiarity, correct alternatives, evaluation criteria, value visibility, channel access, sales friction, education cost, and strategic control. Use an existing category when it provides helpful context.

## Category-Creation Burden Test

Create or redefine a category only when existing categories consistently hide the value and the company can fund education, align ecosystem language, provide proof, and sustain the effort. Model category education as a strategic investment, not a naming exercise.

## Decision-Layer Separation

- **Positioning:** the current market context in which the product wins.
- **Strategy:** choices about where and how the company competes.
- **Vision:** the future state the company seeks to create.
- **Messaging:** expression of the chosen position for an audience and channel.

## Sales Narrative

```text
market insight
-> current alternatives and limitations
-> desired future
-> differentiated value
-> capabilities and proof
-> objections and next decision
```

Adapt the story to the buyer without changing unsupported positioning claims.

## Positioning-Led Qualification

Test whether the prospect has the relevant problem, current alternative, constraints, urgency, priorities, workflow, authority, budget, and readiness to realize differentiated value. Measure qualified progression, win/loss, sales cycle, implementation, activation, and retention.

## Portfolio Positioning Architecture

Choose among independent product positions, an umbrella company position with product-specific value, a platform with modules, or segment-specific positions built on shared capabilities. Evaluate buying context, shared value, cross-sell, category coherence, and dilution risk.

## Positioning Execution

Validate through audience quality, comprehension, sales progression, objection patterns, price acceptance, expected use case, activation, retention, and win/loss reasons. Use message testing as supporting evidence, not the complete test.

## AI Positioning Evidence Audit

Do not use “AI-powered” as assumed differentiation. Compare data, workflow fit, output quality, reliability, control, security, compliance, integration, switching cost, ecosystem, service, and verified outcomes. Identify the real alternative and the evidence required for trust.
