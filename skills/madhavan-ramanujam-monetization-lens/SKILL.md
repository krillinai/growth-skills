# Madhavan Ramanujam Monetization Lens

Apply Madhavan Ramanujam's public methods to product and AI monetization. This is a method lens, not an impersonation, endorsement, or substitute for customer, financial, legal, tax, or accounting evidence.

## Establish the Monetization Unit

Collect product, segment, user, buyer, payer, problem, current alternative, value event, willingness-to-pay evidence, capabilities, packages, price metric, price level, revenue model, usage, outcomes, costs, margin, conversion, retention, expansion, sales motion, and decision. Ask only for consequential gaps.

Keep unknowns explicit. Never invent willingness to pay, price points, customer value, costs, financial forecasts, company facts, or Ramanujam's views.

## Select the Lens

Read [frameworks.md](references/frameworks.md). Choose one primary and at most two supporting lenses.

| Symptom | Primary lens |
| --- | --- |
| The team wants a price after the product is already scoped | Price-before-product audit |
| Interviews show interest but little purchase behavior | Willingness-to-pay evidence |
| One offer serves customers with different needs and value | Value segmentation |
| Customers cannot choose among plans | Packaging architecture |
| Usage grows but bills feel unfair or unpredictable | Price-metric design |
| Revenue model is copied from category convention | Revenue-model selection |
| A product is overbuilt, underpriced, ignored, or kept alive | Four failure modes |
| Free users create cost without a credible upgrade path | Freemium guardrails |
| Revenue grows while margin or retention deteriorates | Profitable-growth model |
| AI pricing debates subscription, usage, and outcomes | Autonomy-attribution audit |
| Growth depends only on new logos | Market-share and wallet-share map |

## Build the Diagnosis

1. Define the customer problem, alternative, value, and decision scope.
2. Separate user, buyer, payer, and value beneficiary.
3. Label evidence `verified`, `inferred`, or `missing`.
4. Map segment, package, price metric, price level, revenue model, and economics as separate decisions.
5. Compare models by value alignment, predictability, measurability, control, expansion, cost, margin, trust, implementation, and risk allocation.
6. Select one primary monetization constraint and preserve alternatives.
7. Recommend three bounded 30-day research or implementation actions.
8. Define a validation plan with population, method, primary evidence, guardrails, maturity, and decision rule.
9. State blind spots and an alternative lens.

Do not output a precise price without credible evidence. Do not use competitor prices as the only anchor, treat stated willingness as demand, choose usage pricing because the product uses AI, or call revenue growth healthy without retained contribution.

## Deliver

Use this output order:

1. **Scope and monetization decision**
2. **Lens applicability**
3. **Customer value and alternative map**
4. **Willingness-to-pay evidence ledger**
5. **Segment and packaging recommendation**
6. **Price metric and revenue-model alternatives**
7. **Economics, trust, and implementation guardrails**
8. **Three prioritized 30-day actions**
9. **Validation plan and decision rules**
10. **Blind spots and unresolved evidence**
11. **Sources**

## Required Boundary

> This analysis applies frameworks documented in Madhavan Ramanujam's public work. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or a simulation of Madhavan Ramanujam.

Never write as Ramanujam, imitate his voice, imply private knowledge, or claim he would choose a price or model.

## Common Mistakes

- Asking customers to approve a price instead of studying tradeoffs and behavior.
- Segmenting only by company size or demographics.
- Combining packaging, price metric, price level, and revenue model into one decision.
- Choosing an expansion metric that discourages valuable use.
- Treating outcome pricing as feasible when attribution or control is weak.
- Ignoring discounts, serving cost, implementation, disputes, churn, and contraction.
