# Mechanisms And Evidence

## Mechanism Families

Use a family as a hypothesis, not a badge:

| Family | Candidate value change | Required evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct or same-side | More relevant peers improve communication, collaboration, identity, status, or compatibility | Interaction success, role value, repeat shared workflows, quality, retention, congestion |
| Cross-side | More relevant participation on one side improves value for another | Availability, qualified demand, match, completion, quality, cross-side retention, economics |
| Marketplace | Local density and trusted exchange improve selection, speed, price, fulfillment, or reliability | Atomic units, active supply, requests, match time, completion, repeat, contribution, multi-homing |
| Content or audience | More qualified creators or consumers improve selection, discovery, feedback, or creator value | Accepted quality, relevance, distribution, creator and consumer retention, moderation, concentration |
| Reputation or identity | Participation creates trusted history that improves decisions or coordination | Accuracy, coverage, portability, correction, appeals, fraud, fairness, trust, retained value |
| Compatibility or standards | Adoption improves interoperability or complements | Successful integrations, complement quality, versioning, switching, governance, fragmentation |
| Data or learning | Permissioned interactions improve the product, creating later participant value | Coverage, labels, model version, product change, exposure, accepted outcomes, cost, drift, reproducibility |
| Workflow or market network | Repeated professional roles and relationships improve execution and discovery | Role-specific jobs, relationship continuity, quality, authority, portability, repeat value, retention |

Do not sum families into a defensibility score. One product may have positive and negative mechanisms at once.

## Marginal-Value Chain

Map:

```text
added eligible participant or contribution
-> changed relevant availability, selection, information, coordination, trust, or compatibility
-> changed core interaction
-> changed outcome for a named existing or later participant
-> repeated value and retention
-> economics and risk after operating costs
```

At each edge record entity, event or state, denominator, window, maturity, unit, evidence, alternative explanation, and negative externality. A missing edge leaves a hypothesis, not proof.

## Measurement Set

Use measures that fit the declared mechanism:

- eligible and available participation by side and unit;
- qualified demand or interaction opportunity;
- relevant selection, coverage, density, and concentration;
- match, response, completion, success, time to value, cancellation, and failure;
- quality, safety, trust, complaints, moderation, reputation error, and appeals;
- repeat interaction, participant retention, cross-side retention, and network-unit survival;
- full and marginal contribution, incentive and subsidy dependence, support and moderation cost;
- multi-homing, unique supply or relationships, portability, switching, disintermediation, and competitor reproducibility;
- congestion, spam, fraud, adverse selection, crowding, creator dilution, participant economics, and nonparticipant harm.

Show distributions and density bands, not averages alone. Preserve zero-interaction units, failed matches, unavailable supply, inactive seats, low-quality content, refunds, reversals, banned actors, deleted records, and unknowns.

## Liquidity And Quality

Define liquidity only for a compatible unit, side, interaction, eligibility rule, and time window. Useful components include:

```text
available supply rate = qualified available supply / eligible supply
match rate = qualified matches / eligible qualified requests
completion rate = successful completions / qualified matches or requests, declared explicitly
time to value = time from eligible request or entry to successful interaction
repeat rate = mature participants repeating the successful interaction / eligible mature participants
```

No universal density, match, completion, repeat, or response threshold exists. A high rate can coexist with low volume, poor selection, unsafe interactions, subsidies, concentration, weak participant economics, or off-platform leakage.

## Defense Tests

Ask what a competitor must reproduce: unique relevant supply, durable relationships, trusted history, embedded workflows, complement ecosystem, proprietary permissioned learning, local density, operational quality, or governance. Then test:

- how much participation is unique versus multi-homed;
- whether reputation, data, and relationships are portable or trapped;
- whether switching reflects value, habit, contract, friction, or coercion;
- whether frequency and retention keep the network current;
- whether concentration makes the network fragile;
- whether participants transact or coordinate off-platform;
- whether competitors can buy, crawl, generate, or partner for equivalent supply or data;
- whether participant value survives lower subsidies, policy changes, new interfaces, or AI agents.

Historical scale and high switching cost are not sufficient.

## Data And AI Gate

Require permissioned data, representative coverage, trustworthy labels, versioned learning, an observable product change, exposure to that change, accepted customer outcomes, repeat value, later data, and full cost. Audit bias, missing populations, feedback amplification, gaming, drift, privacy, safety, latency, explainability, override, and retirement.

Separate:

1. more activity produces more data;
2. data improves an offline model measure;
3. the changed model improves customer outcomes;
4. improved outcomes change acquisition or retention;
5. competitors cannot reproduce the improvement.

Evidence for one step does not prove the next.
