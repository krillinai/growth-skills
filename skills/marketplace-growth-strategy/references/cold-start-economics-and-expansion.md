# Cold Start, Economics, And Expansion

## Cold-Start Sequence

1. Define one urgent customer job and complete cross-side interaction.
2. Select the narrowest atomic market where relevant supply and demand can meet.
3. Identify the binding side or transition and plausible non-marketplace alternatives.
4. Choose the smallest seed method that creates real participant value.
5. Operate the interaction manually or with bounded tooling until failure modes are visible.
6. Measure availability, discovery, response, match, fulfillment, quality, repeat, trust, and contribution.
7. Graduate only after evidence, capacity, and safety gates pass.
8. Expand density or adjacent units one controlled step at a time.

Possible seed methods include curated supply, managed matching, scheduled windows, guaranteed demand, precommitted buyers, waitlists, owned inventory, single-player utility, concierge operations, or a narrow channel partnership. Use them as transitional operating choices, not fake liquidity.

## Graduation Gates

Set local gates for:

- sufficient relevant availability and demand coverage;
- acceptable no-result, response, match, and fulfillment distributions;
- quality, complaint, refund, dispute, fraud, and safety floors;
- side-specific repeat and retention at natural frequency;
- provider utilization, earnings or margin, buyer value, and platform contribution;
- support, manual work, incident response, and operating capacity;
- incentive dependence, concentration, multi-homing, and off-platform behavior;
- metric quality, cohort maturity, evidence coverage, and decision ownership.

Use `pilot`, `continue`, `deepen`, `pause`, `stop`, `exit`, or `graduate`. Do not keep failed markets open to preserve launch counts.

## Economic Ledger

Reconcile by side and atomic market:

```text
transaction value
-> recognized platform revenue
- payment, refund, loss, fraud, support, trust and safety, insurance,
   local operations, fulfillment, service, variable infrastructure,
   incentives, guarantees, taxes, and other applicable variable cost
= contribution before shared fixed cost
```

Also show participant acquisition, onboarding, inventory or labor cost, idle capacity, utilization, earnings or margin, cash timing, working capital, retention, and multi-homing. Calculate only from supplied compatible assumptions and state excluded costs.

Use marginal economics for scale. Average historical contribution can hide unprofitable new supply, categories, times, or cities. GMV and take rate do not establish platform contribution or participant viability.

## Incentive Boundaries

An incentive can remove temporary coordination friction; it cannot prove demand, supply quality, retained value, or Product-Market Fit.

Define barrier, side, eligible population, exposure, action, fulfillment, organic counterfactual, full cost, quality, fraud, cannibalization, pull-forward, post-incentive behavior, retained incremental contribution, budget, cap, taper, pause, and stop.

Do not count all rewarded activity as incremental. Preserve timing, market, category, channel, and organic displacement. Route mechanism, fairness, fulfillment, and portfolio details to `incentive-system-design` and causal design to `experiment-design`.

## Multi-Homing And Disintermediation

Separate participant repeat value from exclusivity. Diagnose why participants use multiple marketplaces or transact directly:

- discovery and matching value;
- relationship ownership and repeat frequency;
- fees, price, convenience, payment, insurance, support, trust, and workflow;
- data and reputation portability;
- participant bargaining power and supply or demand concentration;
- whether the platform remains valuable after the first match.

Favor legitimate ongoing value and proportionate protections. Do not trap reputation, block lawful portability, conceal terms, coerce exclusivity, or treat punitive switching friction as healthy retention or defense.

## Replication And Bridging

A working market is a prior, not proof. Revalidate target customer need, participant roles, frequency, supply shape, geography, time, category, price, competition, platform access, regulation, trust, economics, staffing, and capacity.

For every target unit, define readiness, local owner, seed method, service promise, quality floor, economic boundary, support, launch, graduation, pause, stop, rollback, and learning gates. Keep failed and exited markets visible.

Bridge markets, inventory, identity, reputation, or demand only when cross-unit participation improves participant value and preserves local diagnosability. Aggregate coverage must not hide weak local liquidity.

## Case Transfer

Record source, speaker, publication date, company period, artifact type, claim, limitation, and transfer boundary. Separate historical fact, participant account, editorial synthesis, transferable principle, implementation detail, and unsupported inference.

Uber supports hypotheses about local density, hard-side supply, utilization, incentives, city operations, and replication. It does not supply universal ratios, thresholds, prices, staffing, causal effects, or current best practice. Airbnb supports a productized distribution pattern but not the incremental contribution of one channel. Use cases only to sharpen a local question or evidence plan.
