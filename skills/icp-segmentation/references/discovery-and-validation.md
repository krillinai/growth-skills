# Discovery And Validation

## Decision-First Discovery

1. Choose one material product, positioning, acquisition, sales, service, monetization, or retention decision.
2. Define the unit and role hierarchy.
3. Gather evidence across successful, failed, churned, non-converting, low-burden, and exceptional-service cases.
4. Form a small number of hypotheses using conditions that could be known when the decision occurs.
5. Specify different actions and shared defaults.
6. Compare compatible mature value and economic outcomes.
7. Test whether the differentiated action improves downstream value.
8. keep, merge, split, revise, or retire the segment.

Exploratory clustering can suggest hypotheses. Statistical separation alone does not make a group strategically useful.

## Comparison Matrix

Compare candidate segments on population and absolute volume; qualified first value and time; value-bearing frequency, breadth, depth, and collaboration; user, account, transaction, supply, demand, or revenue retention; renewal, expansion, contraction, and resurrection; price and willingness-to-pay evidence; acquisition, sales cycle, implementation, support, fulfillment, inference, and failure cost; contribution, payback, concentration, trust, and harm.

Freeze entity, eligibility, event, denominator, window, maturity, identity, currency, cost scope, and source. Do not combine incompatible units or conclude causality from observed differences.

## Prospective Identification

For each rule record when the feature becomes available, coverage, unknown rate, stability, false-positive and false-negative costs, precision, recall, calibration where relevant, manual override, and fallback. Avoid outcome leakage: later retention can label a training or analysis set, but production routing needs pre-treatment features and forward validation.

## Path Architecture

Prefer:

```text
shared default
+ evidence-backed conditional guidance
+ assisted route for justified complexity or value
+ explicit exclusion or waitlist where value cannot be delivered
```

Every path needs an entry rule, promise, experience, owner, capacity, measurement window, exit, fallback, collision rule, and stop condition. Remove paths whose incremental value does not justify complexity.

## Model Boundary

A propensity model estimates a defined outcome under historical conditions. It does not explain value, establish an ICP, or identify incremental treatment response. Evaluate population, features available before treatment, label quality, leakage, calibration, precision, recall, group error, drift, feedback, capacity, overrides, and causal validation.

## Governance Review

Review decision usefulness, reproducibility, unknowns, overlaps, misclassification, evidence disagreement, path lift, complexity, privacy, fairness, market change, and owner action. Revalidate after changes to product, price, channel, market, competition, customer mix, capacity, or applicable rules.
