# Packages And Price Metrics

Packages define what a customer can buy and use. Price metrics define what changes the bill. Design both from stable customer value and operating reality rather than feature count or category convention.

## Package Architecture

For each decision-relevant segment map:

```text
job, alternative, and outcome
-> core value and proof
-> capabilities and entitlements
-> quantity, control, service, and risk limits
-> package and optional add-ons
-> upgrade, downgrade, expansion, and exit
```

Record package purpose, eligible and excluded customers, core value, differentiated value, entitlements, limits, included units, overage, service level, controls, integrations, data, support, implementation, compatibility, add-ons, provisioning, migration, and retirement.

Use one package across segments when the buying and value pattern is genuinely shared. Add a package only when it creates a stable, explainable, sellable, billable, supportable decision path. Avoid package-per-request proliferation, random Good-Better-Best feature counts, opaque decoys, excessive add-ons, and custom terms that silently become the product.

## Trust And Essential Capability

Evaluate security, privacy, accessibility, reliability, data export and portability, customer control, promised support, legal or contractual requirements, and safety as trust or eligibility constraints. Do not gate an essential right or baseline promised protection merely to coerce an upgrade. Separate legitimate service and risk differences from artificial deprivation.

## Price-Metric Scorecard

Compare candidates without collapsing them into an unsupported single score:

| Dimension | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Customer value | Does the unit rise with a realized outcome the customer recognizes? |
| Payer value | Can the buyer and payer understand and accept the relationship? |
| Measurement | Is the unit defined, observable, auditable, and correctable? |
| Predictability | Can customers forecast and control spend with caps and alerts? |
| Cost | Does revenue remain compatible with delivery, service, risk, and capacity? |
| Expansion | Does successful adoption create natural growth without suppressing use? |
| Incentives | Does the unit encourage value rather than gaming, waste, or avoidance? |
| Fairness | Are comparable situations treated consistently and explainably? |
| Operations | Can product, metering, billing, finance, sales, and support run it reliably? |

Preserve counterevidence and trade-offs. A highly value-correlated outcome can fail because attribution is disputed. A precise usage unit can fail because bills are unpredictable. Seats can fail when collaboration creates value but broad access is penalized.

## Common Metrics

- `seat or active user`: useful when individual access or active participation maps to value; risky for shared or collaborative workflows;
- `usage or consumption`: useful when consumption maps to value and cost; risky when activity includes failure, retry, or waste;
- `transaction or take rate`: useful for enabled exchange; risky when leakage, participant margins, or liquidity weaken;
- `asset, location, workspace, workflow, or data unit`: useful when the unit is stable and auditable; risky when proxies diverge from outcome;
- `outcome`: useful only when result, attribution, control, quality, timing, dispute, and payment are accepted;
- `subscription or flat fee`: predictable and simple; risky when value and cost vary widely;
- `hybrid`: can reconcile base access, use, and service; complexity must earn its place.

Do not copy competitors or upstream providers. Their unit may reflect a different customer, product, cost, market, risk, or business model.

## Price Structure And Terms

For each bounded proposal define metric unit, included amount, floor, ceiling, band, overage, cap, alert, minimum, maximum, commitment, billing cadence, renewal, prepayment, installment, currency, regional version, tax and invoice handoffs, discount, credit, service, refund, cancellation, downgrade, exception, and expiry.

Keep visible list price, quoted price, contracted terms, billed amount, collected cash, and realized revenue separate. Reconcile free periods, discounts, credits, channel margins, services, refunds, non-standard terms, and concessions. A discount can exchange value for commitment, volume, timing, proof, or reduced service; evaluate its purpose, evidence, give-get, retained outcome, contribution, approval, and expiry rather than declaring every discount good or bad.

## Free, Trial, Freemium, And Pilot

Define eligibility, access, time or use boundary, value event, natural frequency, activation, repeat value, conversion opportunity, payer event, serving cost, support, fraud and abuse, distribution value, collaboration value, upgrade reason, fallback, cancellation, and retained payer quality.

A short trial fails when value or procurement takes longer. Freemium fails when free users receive little value or produce unsustainable cost without distribution, learning, or upgrade value. A paid pilot fails when custom implementation never becomes a repeatable product path. Do not remove free access or move a paywall from conversion alone.

## AI Pricing

Map successful task or workflow, outcome quality, latency, model and tool usage, retries, failures, human review, support, privacy, vendor and model version, unit cost, cost volatility, capacity, budget predictability, caps, alerts, disputes, and customer control.

Tokens, calls, compute, or agent actions are cost and activity inputs, not automatically customer value. Compare subscription, included usage, credits, workflow units, successful tasks, outcomes, and hybrids through the scorecard. Define quality and failure treatment before charging for output.

## Marketplaces And Multi-Sided Products

Keep buyer, seller, creator, consumer, advertiser, supplier, transaction, order, GMV, take-rate revenue, incentive, refund, contribution, liquidity, quality, and multi-homing separate. Define who pays, who receives value, which side is constrained, how a fee changes participation, and whether one side's subsidy is another side's acquisition cost.

Do not add participant sides into one package or customer economics model without an explicit joint-value and reconciliation contract.
